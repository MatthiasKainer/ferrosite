The first symptom is usually a website problem, but the actual issue runs
deeper. A service-heavy company starts hearing the same questions from buyers:

- What exactly do you sell?
- How is this different from consulting?
- What happens after we say yes?

When those questions pile up, the problem is rarely a missing landing page. It
is usually a signal that the market story, offer design, and internal delivery
model are out of sync.

## Start with the operating promise

Every strong B2B company has an operating promise. It is the sentence that
explains what changes for the buyer once the work is live. Not the methodology.
Not the list of services. The actual before-and-after state.

If that promise is unclear internally, the website will only amplify the
confusion. Sales will improvise. Marketing will over-explain. Delivery will
inherit work that never should have been sold in the first place.

## Then rebuild the path

Once the promise is clear, the web experience should guide buyers through the
few decisions that matter:

1. Is this for a company like ours?
2. Which problem should we start with?
3. What happens if we engage?

Clarity on those three questions does more for revenue quality than another
dozen proof points scattered across the site.

## What good looks like

A strong company website is not just persuasive. It is operational. It helps
buyers self-select, helps internal teams stay aligned, and reduces the amount
of explanation required in every later conversation.
